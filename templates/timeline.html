International timeline

A short chronology of the battle of the superpowers

events explanation pictures
1945 – Nuclear scare

The USA drops 2 nuclear bombs in Japan. The new weapon characterizes the Cold War.
1947 – Marshall-Plan

Washington finances the reconstruction of Western Europe with Billions to confine the influence of Russia.

1947 – Secret Services

The USA are founding the CIA; the USSR creates in 1954 the KGB. Espionage becomes a central weapon.

1949 – Block-Building

The most important West-States establish NATO; Moscow counters with the Warsaw Pact.

1950 – Korea War

The conflict in Eastern Asia escalates to a confrontation of the two superpowers creating millions of victims.

1956 – Revolt in Hungary

Russian military beats down the protest of Budapest. The West is only watching, as it doesn’t want to risk a war.

1961 – Divided City

With the Berlin Wall the GDR builds the symbol of Cold War. It didn’t fall until 1989.

1962 – Cuba-Crisis

When the USA discover the Russian missile base in Cuba; the threat of a nuclear war impends.

1965 – Vietnam War

The attempt to evict the communists out of Vietnam escalates to become a disastrous war for the USA.

1968 – Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty

Washington, London and Moscow agree / arrange not to deliver nuclear warheads to other countries anymore.

1969 – Space-Race

With the moon landing, the USA takes the lead in the billion-dollar Space Race with the USSR.

1979 – NATO-double-track decision

In order to force an agreement on disarmament, NATO is stationing new missiles in Europe.

1979 – Afghanistan

The Russian invasion in Afghanistan intensified the arms race, that ruins Moscow in the end.

1990 – Reunion

The GDR joins the FRG – so the most explosive frontier in the conflict disappeared.

1991 – Collapse of Eastern Bloc

The fall of the USSR finishes the 4 ½ Decades of the Cold War.

1944 – The Bretton Woods conference

Although representatives of 44 Allied nations attended this conference to determine a world order post WWII, it was the UK and USA that led the way establishing the World Bank, IMF and am exchange rate system that would ensure the success of western liberal democracies and was partly to counter Stalinist ideologies. Influenced by the UK Economist Keynes.
The Economist, 2014
1946 – The “Iron Curtain”

"From Stettin in the Baltic to Trieste in the Adriatic an "iron curtain" has descended across the continent. Churchill had always distrusted the Soviets at Yalta and elsewhere and had hoped Europe would have parliamentary democracies as Russia had promised. The Russian view of democracy was very different to that held in the west.
Westminster College
1948 – Berlin Airlift

In 1948 the soviets tried to starve Berlin into a single city under Soviet control. The west was determined to see a reunified Germany with berlin as capital and were not prepared to see berlin fall into Soviet hands, so the airlift began.
Trumanlibrary
1949 – Formation of NATO

Marshall aid was attempting to make Europe wealthy and NATO was needed to provide security against possible expansion by the USSR. Strength via collective strength provided an effective coalition.
Wikimedia
1953 – Korean war

After the North invaded South Korea in 195o the UK supported the USA in its appeal to the UN to send troops. Almost 100,000 UK troops fought in the campaign at some time in terrible conditions. The real danger was that China backed the north and fear for a new World war was high. The USA had adopted a policy to contain the spread of communism so the south could not be allowed to fall.
Wikimedia
1956 – Suez Crisis

Although independent Egypt was a British protectorate. Growing nationalism after WWII fueled by Soviet money lead Egypt to nationalize the canal. Britain and France sent troops but in a post war austere period with dwindling influence there was no real heart to maintain control of the canal despite its importance for trade.
Yorkshirepost
1973 – UK enters the EEC

The move to join the European community, finally allowed the UK to realise Empire was gone and that its future (short term at least) lay in closer relations with Europe. Until this time, Britain had been continuing to behave as if it was still a superpower.
Keystone-France/Gamma-Keystone via Getty Images
1979 – Margaret Thatcher becomes Prime Minister

Thatcher quickly allied with president Reagan in the years 80-88 to form a western alliance that was less tolerant of Russia. Expenditure on military increased and it was Russia’s inability to keep pace that helped to contribute to its decline
Frontiers of freedom
1981 – Greenham Common Peace camps

The rise of CND and the anti-nuclear movement was given support by hundreds of women forming a peaceful camp at the American airbase at Greenham where US nuclear missiles were allegedly kept on UK soil, making the area a target for a Soviet strike. Not all were happy with the UK policy of following the US lead on all matters.
Telegraph
events explanation pictures
July 17th – August 2nd, 1945 – Potsdam Conference

After the war Germany and its capital Berlin is divided into four zones. They are directed by military governors of the victorious powers USA, GB, F, USSR.
June 24th, 1948 – May 1949 – Berlin Blockade

Russia’s response to the merge of the partitions of Berlin: All roads and rail links to that sector are cut. Therefore, the inhabitants of Berlin have no access to food supplies and faced starvation. The Berlin Airlift brings food to West Berlin with permanent flights of British and US-American airplanes.
May 23rd, 1949 – Foundation of the FRG

Due to a dissent over the question of Germany, the French, US and British zones form West Germany.
October 7th, 1949 – Founding of the GDR

As a response to both dissent over the German question and the unsuccessful Berlin Blockade, Russia urges the Eastern governors to found the GDR.
June 17th, 1953 – Uprising of the East German People

More than 40,000 citizens of the GRD protest against the socialist system. The government suppresses the protesters with the help of Soviet tanks. About 55 people are killed that day and afterwards.
August 13th, 1961 – Erection of Berlin Wall

To keep people from running away from the East, the Berlin Wall is built and borders are sealed between East and West Germany.
December 7th, 1970 – Willy Brandt’s Warsaw Genuflection

During a visit in Warsaw the German Chancellor Willy Brandt kneels down in front of a monument to the victims of the Nazi regime. Hence he introduces a policy of détente (easing of tension).
1975 – CSCE Helsinki (Conference for Security and Cooperation in Europe)

Primetime of policy of détente (easing of tension)
December 12th, 1979 – NATO Bilateral Agreement

Reoccurrence of Cold War features by deciding a rocket deployment (Pershing 2) as response to the Russian deployment of their SS 20 rockets
November 9th, 1989 – Fall of Berlin Wall and Reunification of Germany as a consequence of Gorbachev’s policy of perestroika (rebuilding) and glasnost (transparency)

The speaker of the GDR government reads out that citizens of the GDR can cross the border, so that afterwards thousands of GDR citizens are welcomed to the west by crowds of cheering West-Germans.
October 3rd, 1990

East and West Germany are reunited as one country.
events explanation pictures
February 1948 – The Communist Coup in Czechoslovakia

On 17th - 25th february 1948 the Communist Party of Czechoslovakia, led by Klement Gottwald, officially gained full power over the country. It had taken just three short years for the communists to gain full control of Czechoslovakia following the end of World War II, but, by the standards of other Eastern European countries, they were fairly late in establishing power. This led to a 42 year long communistic regime in Czechoslovakia during which all “inconvenient” people became its victims thanks to political persecutions, forced collectivisation etc.
Wikimedia
June 11, 1960 – Promulgation of Constitution of Czechoslovakia

On 11th June 1960 a new constitution was established. The constitution declared the victory of socialism and proclaimed the Czechoslovak Socialistic Republic. The ambiguous precept of "democratic centralism" — power emanating from the people but bound by the authority of higher organs — was made a formal part of constitutional law. The president, the cabinet, the Slovak National Council, and the local governments were made responsible to the National Assembly. All private enterprises using hired labour were abolished. Comprehensive economic planning was reaffirmed. The Bill of Rights emphasized economic and social rights, e.g., the right to work, leisure, health care, and education. Civil rights, however, were deemphasized.
Wikimedia
August 20, 1968 – Invasion of Czechoslovakia

The year 1968 is registered in the history of Czechoslovakia as an attempt to establish socialism with a human face, which meant an effort to democratize the communist system. Alexander Dubcek, who was elected as First Secretary of the Central Committee, promoted the removal of censorship, freedom of travel, authorization of non-communist societies, efforts to liberate Czechs and Slovaks, and improving the situation of churches. With his human approach, Dubcek gradually gained the sympathy and support of the people. However the Soviet Union looked critically at events in Czechoslovakia. The Soviets addressed several warnings to the Czechoslovak authorities during the year 1968 to changes in the internal political line. As the Czechoslovak leadership did not do so, they decided to overthrow the development by military action. The occupation began on the night of August 20, 1968. Despite the denial of armed resistance of the Communist leadership, unarmed people in many places spontaneously confronted Soviet tanks. Soldiers responded by firing into groups of protestors in several cities, which resulted in many casualties. The occupation stopped the process of democratization and slowly a period of normalization that abolished all the achievements of socialism with the human face began.
Wikimedia
January 1, 1977 – Charter 77

Charter 77 was a civic initiative from 1976 to 1992, named after the document Charter 77 from January 1977. Charter 77 criticized the government for failing to implement human rights provisions in a number of documents it had signed, including the 1960 Constitution of Czechoslovakia, the Final Act of the 1975 Conference on Security and Cooperation in Europe and 1966 United Nations covenants on political, civil, economic, and cultural rights. Spreading the text of the document was considered a political crime.
Wikimedia (Author: Gampe)
March 25, 1988 – The Candle Manifestation

The Candle manifestation (also called Bratislava Good Friday) took place at Hviezdoslav square in Bratislava on March 25, 1988. It was a peaceful gathering of 10 000 citizens who were demonstrating for religious freedom and civil rights. People, who participated in the demonstration had been standing in the square with burning candles and praying before the security forces arrived there. People did not leave the place so therefore policemen started a brutal attack. They used water cannons to disperse people. During this “peaceful manifestation” 14 people were injured and 141 citizens were arrested and sent for questioning.
Wikimedia
November 17, 1989 – The Velvet Revolution

On November 17, 1989, students and artists came to Prague to recall the 20th anniversary of the death of Ján Palach and brutal Nazi intervention against the Czech students in 1939. The peaceful protest was interrupted by the members of security forces who started to disperse the students. 538 students were injured during this revolution and some of them were arrested. These events led to the fall of the 42-year lasting communist regime in Czechoslovakia and to the break-up of Czechoslovakia.
Wikimedia
June 8 - June 9, 1990 – First free elections

On June 8 and June 9,1990 the first federal elections after the Velvet revolution and the first free elections since 1946 were held. More than 3 milion people came to vote. The Political party The Public Against Violence (VPN) with 29,35% won 48 of the 150 seats and Vladimír Mečiar became the Prime Minister. The second place political party was the Christian Democratic Movement (KDH) with 19,21 % won 31 seats, The VPN formed a coalition with the KDH.
Wikimedia
January 1, 1993 – Formation of the Slovak Republic

On January 1, 1993, the Slovak Republic was established when the Slovak Government and the National Council of the Slovak Republic adopted a declaration of the establishment of an independent Slovak Republic. In the Czech Republic and Slovakia, the presidential election took place. In Prague, Vaclav Havel was elected. The first Slovak President was Michal Kováč. An agreement on the association of the Slovak Republic with the European Community was signed in Luxemburg this year and the Czech Republic and the Slovak Republic became members of the Council of Europe.
Wikimedia

1939 – End of the Civil War. Franco’s dictatorship

The aftermath of the civil war saw Spain governed by an authoritarian and conservative dictatorship under General Franco. Opposition voices were repressed through prison and execution. Franco’s Spain stayed largely neutral in World War 2, allowing the regime to survive until Franco’s death in 1975. By its end, the regime was increasingly at odds with a Spain which had been culturally transformed.
1953 – USA sets military bases in Spain

USA needed to have military bases in Europe to threaten the USSR, and also allies to fight communism. For this reason Eisenhower decided to make a pact with general Franco (Pactos de Madrid) and restore the relationship with Spain, breaking its international isolation. Later, in the 60s, the economic growth and the arrival of tourists allowed for the progressive modernization of the country.
1973 – ETA murders Admiral Carrero Blanco, President of the Government

Born at the end of the 1950s, this organization aimed for the independence of the Basque country. From the end of the 1960s, they used violence to achieve their objectives, and in 1973 they murdered Admiral Carrero Blanco, Franco’s right-hand man. In the 1980s and 1990s there were terrible bomb attacks against both military and civilian objectives, like the one against the Guardia Civil in Plaza de República Dominicana (Madrid) or the one against the Hipercor supermarket in Barcelona
1975 – Franco dies. Coronation of Juan Carlos I

When Franco died in November 1975, he was succeeded, as the government had planned in 1969, by Juan Carlos Borbón, the heir to the vacant throne. The new king was committed to democracy, and careful negotiation, as well as the presence of a modern society looking for freedom, allowed for a referendum on political reform. Adolfo Suarez, the first President of the Government, managed a relatively smooth transition to stable democracy.
1978 – Spanish Constitution

After legalizing the Communist Party, which was forbidden during Franco’s dictatorship, all the political leaders worked to reach a consensus and build a modern state. The new constitution, which confirmed Spain as a parliamentary monarchy, was approved by 88% of the population. The swift switch from dictatorship to democracy became an example for post-communist Eastern Europe.
1981 – Military Coup d’Etat

On February 23th, 1981, there was a military coup d’Etat. Lieutenant Colonel Tejero, with 200 armed soldiers, entered the Congreso de los Diputados during the election of the new President of the Government. The outgoing President, Adolfo Suárez and one of his ministers tried to defend democracy against this attack. After being informed, the king Juan Carlos addressed the Spanish people on TV, supporting the Constitution. His firm defense of democracy helped to put an end to the coup.
1986 – Spain enters the EU

After its successful transition to democracy, as well as quite a few strict economic measures, Spain joined the European Economic Community, later to become the European Union. In this way, we became a more modern and richer country. Since 2002 we have been part of the euro-zone.
1992 – World Expo and Olympic Games

To commemorate the 500-hundred anniversary of the discovery of America, Spain hosted many world events, like the World Exposition in Seville, the European Culture capital for Madrid (with the opening of the Thyssen museum), and, above all, the huge success of the Olympic Games in Barcelona. All these events showed to the world that Spain was a modern and prosperous country, helping to overcome stereotypes from other times.
1997 – ETA murders Miguel Ángel Blanco

The 48-hour kidnapping and subsequent murder of Basque councilman Miguel Ángel Blanco started a civic movement against the terrorist band and its social support. From 2000, the attacks decreased, and after losing political influence in Euskadi, in 2011 ETA put an end to their violent activity.
